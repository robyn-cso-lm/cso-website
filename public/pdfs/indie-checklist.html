Welcome

You found your surrogate.
Now what?

This checklist is for families who are doing their surrogacy journey independently - meaning you have found your surrogate yourself, whether through a friend, family member, online community, or your own network. You are not working with a full-service agency, or you are using limited agency support.

That is a completely valid path. But it is also a path where things can go wrong when steps are missed, done in the wrong order, or handled without the right professionals involved.

I have been in this field since I was four years old. My mother founded Canadian Surrogacy Options in 1992. I have seen independent journeys go beautifully - and I have seen them fall apart because someone skipped a step that seemed minor at the time.

"This checklist will not replace a lawyer, a clinic, or a counsellor. But it will make sure you know what you need and when you need it."

Work through this guide in order. Every phase builds on the last. And if at any point you feel like you need more support than a checklist provides, CSO offers consultation calls, receipt management, and independent journey support packages - you do not have to do this completely alone.

- Robyn Price, Executive Director, Canadian Surrogacy Options

01
Phase One
Confirming Readiness
Before anything else is signed or scheduled
Intended Parents
  • You have a confirmed diagnosis or medical reason for surrogacy
  • You have completed or are working with a fertility clinic on embryo creation
  • You have a realistic understanding of total costs ($60,000 to $150,000+ CAD) Priority
  • You have confirmed access to funds or financing before proceeding Priority
  • You have discussed surrogacy in depth with your partner if applicable
  • You have or are arranging psychological support for yourself
  • You understand the Canadian altruistic model - no fees to surrogate, reimbursements only Priority
Your Surrogate
  • She has had at least one previous successful pregnancy and birth Priority
  • She is between 21 and 45 years of age
  • She is a Canadian citizen or permanent resident
  • She is not currently on social assistance or government benefits Priority

Receiving reimbursements while on benefits can create legal complications.

  • She has discussed surrogacy with her partner or support person
  • She has access to independent legal counsel Priority
  • She understands she will need independent psychological screening
  • She is not being coerced or pressured by anyone to be a surrogate Priority

02
Phase Two
Building Your Team
The professionals you cannot skip
Surrogacy Lawyer (for IPs)
Required
You need a lawyer who specialises in reproductive law in your province. They will draft and review the surrogacy agreement and handle the parentage order after birth. Do not use a general family lawyer for this.
Independent Lawyer (for Surrogate)
Required
Your surrogate must have her own independent legal counsel - separate from yours. This is a legal and ethical requirement. You typically pay for her legal fees.
Fertility Clinic
Required
The clinic performs medical screening of your surrogate, manages the embryo transfer, and handles all medical coordination. Your surrogate must be cleared by your clinic before any agreement is signed.
Registered Psychologist or Counsellor
Required
Independent psychological screening for the surrogate (and ideally IPs) is required by most clinics and strongly recommended by lawyers. Use a professional with reproductive experience.
Life Insurance Broker
Required
Your surrogate needs life insurance for the duration of the pregnancy. Some standard policies exclude surrogacy - get this confirmed in writing before the transfer.
Accountant
Optional - Recommended
Surrogate reimbursements, trust accounts, and cross-border payments can have tax implications. A brief consultation is worth it.

Phase Three
Medical Screening
Before any legal agreements are signed
Important Order of Operations

Medical screening happens before legal agreements. Do not sign anything until your surrogate has been medically cleared by your clinic.

Surrogate Medical Screening
  • Surrogate has had initial consultation with your fertility clinic Priority
  • Full physical exam and bloodwork completed
  • Uterine assessment (sonohysterogram or hysteroscopy) completed Priority
  • Infectious disease screening completed for surrogate and her partner Priority
  • Psychological evaluation by registered professional completed Priority
  • Drug and alcohol screening completed if required by clinic
  • Clinic has issued formal medical clearance in writing Priority
  • Surrogate's OB/GYN has been identified for pregnancy care
Embryo / Genetic Material
  • Embryos are created and confirmed viable by your clinic Priority
  • Embryo grading and quantity confirmed in writing from clinic
  • Embryos are stored at a clinic that can coordinate with your surrogate's transfer clinic Priority

If embryos are at a different clinic (e.g. Montreal for Quebec IPs), confirm transfer logistics early.

  • Genetic testing (PGT) status confirmed and documented
  • Embryo transport arrangements confirmed if applicable

Phase Four
Legal Agreements
The most important phase - do not rush this
Non-negotiable rule

The surrogacy agreement is the legal foundation of your entire journey. It must be completed and signed by all parties before any embryo transfer takes place. No exceptions.

  • Both parties have retained independent legal counsel Priority
  • IPs' lawyer has drafted the surrogacy agreement Priority
  • Surrogate's lawyer has reviewed the agreement independently Priority

Allow at least 2 to 4 weeks for this process. Do not rush your surrogate's lawyer review.

  • Agreement includes: reimbursement schedule and categories
  • Agreement includes: selective reduction and termination clauses Priority
  • Agreement includes: multi-fetal pregnancy terms
  • Agreement includes: contact and communication expectations during pregnancy
  • Agreement includes: what happens if the surrogate or IPs need to withdraw Priority
  • Agreement includes: travel restrictions and lifestyle expectations
  • Agreement includes: media and social media restrictions if applicable
  • All parties have signed the agreement before transfer proceeds Priority
  • Copies of signed agreement held by both legal teams
Province Note - Quebec

If your surrogate is in Quebec, note that Quebec's Civil Code does not recognise surrogacy agreements as enforceable. Use a surrogate in another province. Your lawyer can advise.

Phase Five
Financial Setup
Protecting your surrogate and yourself

How you manage money on an independent journey matters enormously - legally, ethically, and practically. Here is what needs to be in place before any reimbursements flow.

Trust Account and Expense Management
  • A trust account has been established to hold surrogate expense funds Priority

This should be managed by a lawyer, not by you or the surrogate directly. CSO also offers receipt management services if you need a third party to handle this.

  • Initial trust deposit made per agreement terms
  • Surrogate has been provided with a copy of approved expense categories Priority
  • Receipt submission process agreed upon and documented
  • Reimbursement turnaround time agreed upon (recommended: within 14 days of receipt)
  • Surrogate life insurance policy in place and active Priority
  • Disability insurance reviewed if applicable
  • Surrogate's base monthly allowance (if applicable) confirmed and scheduled
Important

Never pay your surrogate directly in cash or e-transfer without documentation. Every dollar should be traceable, documented, and receipted. This protects both of you.

Approved Reimbursement Categories

The following expenses are generally considered reimbursable under Canadian law when documented with receipts:

  • Maternity clothing allowance
  • Travel to and from medical appointments (mileage, parking, transit)
  • Childcare during medical appointments
  • Lost wages for mandatory medical appointments
  • Medications and supplements prescribed for the surrogacy
  • Psychological counselling during the journey
  • Life and disability insurance premiums
  • Legal fees for the surrogate's independent counsel
  • Housekeeping if medically required post-transfer or post-birth
  • Hospital costs not covered by provincial health insurance
  • Comfort items and nutritional supplements as agreed

Phase Six
The Pregnancy
Staying connected and keeping things healthy
  • Positive pregnancy test confirmed - both parties notified
  • First trimester screening appointment dates shared with IPs
  • Communication schedule agreed upon (calls, texts, appointment updates)
  • IPs have been introduced to surrogate's OB/GYN and are on approved contact list

Ask the OB/GYN office in writing to copy IPs on appointment summaries.

  • Anatomy scan (18 to 20 weeks) attended or report shared with IPs
  • Monthly expense reimbursements processed on time Priority
  • Any complications or hospitalisation communicated to IPs immediately Priority
  • Third trimester check-in call between all parties completed
  • Psychological support available to surrogate throughout - not just at start
  • IPs have arranged travel plans if they live outside surrogate's province
  • Birth preferences discussed and documented
On the relationship

The relationship between IPs and surrogate is the heart of this journey. Check in regularly, acknowledge milestones, send thank-you notes. The families who build genuine relationships with their surrogates almost always describe their experience as one of the most meaningful of their lives.

Phase Seven
Birth Preparation
The 8 weeks before your due date
  • Hospital birth plan completed and on file with delivering hospital
  • Hospital has been notified of the surrogacy arrangement in writing Priority
  • IPs are listed as authorised individuals at the hospital Priority
  • IPs' lawyer has filed for pre-birth order if applicable in that province Priority

Ontario, BC, and Alberta typically allow pre-birth orders. File at least 6 to 8 weeks before due date.

  • IPs have confirmed travel and accommodation near birth location
  • Emergency contact plan documented - what happens if birth is early
  • Surrogate has a support person for the birth separate from IPs if desired
  • Cord blood banking decision made and kit obtained if applicable
  • Newborn care plan confirmed (who is in the room, who holds baby first, etc.)
  • IPs have reviewed their province's parentage process with their lawyer Priority

Phase Eight
After the Birth
Legal parentage and closing the journey

The baby is here. But the journey is not quite over. Here is what needs to happen in the weeks and months after birth.

  • Birth certificate application filed with IPs listed as parents Priority

In provinces with pre-birth orders this is straightforward. In others, additional steps required.

  • Post-birth parentage order obtained if pre-birth order was not available Priority
  • Passport application initiated for baby if travel is required
  • Final expense reimbursements processed and documented Priority
  • Trust account closed and final accounting provided to both parties Priority
  • Life insurance policy on surrogate terminated (with her confirmation)
  • Any final medical follow-up for surrogate attended and reimbursed
  • Gratitude acknowledged - letter, gift, or agreed-upon token of appreciation
  • Future contact expectations discussed and agreed upon
  • All legal files closed with both legal teams

"Congratulations. You did this. From hope to heartbeat to home - exactly as it should be."

Appendix

The Most Common Independent Journey Mistakes

These are the errors that come up most frequently - and cause the most damage. Read through them once, even if you are confident you know what you are doing.

Starting medical before legal
The embryo transfer happens before the surrogacy agreement is signed. This leaves everyone legally unprotected. Agreement first - always.
Paying the surrogate directly
Cash or e-transfer payments without documentation create legal risk and can be mischaracterised as commercial surrogacy. Use a trust account.
Skipping independent legal counsel for the surrogate
Some IPs try to use one lawyer for both parties to save money. This is legally and ethically unacceptable. Both parties need independent counsel. Period.
Not confirming insurance coverage
Standard life insurance policies sometimes exclude surrogacy. Confirm in writing before the transfer that your surrogate is covered.
Rushing the match because you are emotionally ready
Being emotionally ready to proceed does not mean the surrogate is medically and psychologically cleared. The screening process exists to protect everyone - including her. Do not skip or rush it.
Ignoring provincial parentage laws
Parentage law varies significantly by province. What works in Ontario may not work the same way in another province. Your lawyer needs to be licensed in the relevant province.
Underestimating the cost
$60,000 is the floor, not the ceiling. Budget for complications, additional transfer cycles, and contingencies. Going into this under-resourced is one of the most common causes of journey breakdown.
