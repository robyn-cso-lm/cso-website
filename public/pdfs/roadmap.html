Welcome & How to Use This Guide

You are holding the most comprehensive guide to Canadian surrogacy that exists outside of a lawyer's office. I know that's a big claim. But I have been in this field since I was four years old, and I built this guide because I was tired of watching families navigate one of the most important journeys of their lives with incomplete, confusing, or commercially biased information.

This roadmap covers everything. From the first honest questions you should ask yourself, through matching, legal agreements, the pregnancy, the birth, and the weeks after. Every phase has a detailed guide and the worksheet section at the back gives you fillable tools to use throughout.

Read it in order the first time. Then use individual sections as reference as you move through your journey. The worksheets are designed to be printed and filled in by hand, or typed into directly.

Part Two

Is Surrogacy Right For You?

Before anything else, these are the honest questions worth sitting with. There are no wrong answers — just important ones.

Do you have a clear medical or personal reason?
Surrogacy is pursued for many valid reasons — medical necessity, same-sex family building, single parenthood, and more. You do not need to justify your reason to anyone. But clarity about your why will carry you through the harder moments.
Are you financially prepared?
Canadian surrogacy typically costs $60,000 to $150,000 CAD. Not having the full amount upfront is common — but going in significantly under-resourced creates serious risk. Understand your financial picture before you begin.
Are you emotionally ready to share this journey?
Your surrogate will be deeply involved in one of the most intimate experiences of your life. The families who do best approach this as a relationship, not a transaction.
Do you have support around you?
A partner, trusted friends, a therapist, or a community. Surrogacy journeys have high points and hard points. You should not face either alone.
Can you handle uncertainty and longer timelines?
Matching can take months. Transfers sometimes fail. Timelines slip. The families who navigate this most gracefully are those who went in knowing it could be long.

Part Three

Understanding the Canadian Framework

Canada has one of the clearest legal frameworks for surrogacy in the world. Here is what you need to understand before you start.

The Assisted Human Reproduction Act (AHRA)

  • Altruistic surrogacy is legal across Canada.
  • Commercial surrogacy — paying a surrogate a fee or wage — is prohibited.
  • Surrogates can be reimbursed for documented, pregnancy-related expenses.
  • A surrogacy agreement must be in place before any embryo transfer.
  • Both parties must have independent legal counsel.
  • The agreement is not automatically legally enforceable in all provinces — a parentage order from the court is what legally establishes parenthood.

Provincial Differences

Part Four

Your Complete Journey Timeline

Here is the full surrogacy journey mapped from start to finish. Timeframes are realistic averages — your journey may be faster or slower. Use this as a planning reference, not a guarantee.

01
Weeks 1–4
Choose your agency or independent path
Research agencies, check references, ask hard questions. If going independent, confirm your surrogate is genuinely ready and meets eligibility criteria.
02
Weeks 3–8
Complete intake and build your IP profile
Your agency or your own process: complete intake forms, gather medical history, write your IP profile. This is what surrogates will review when considering a match.
03
Months 1–24
Surrogate matching
The most variable phase. With a priority program: 4–9 months average. With a standard program: 12–24 months. Independent journey with known surrogate: parallel to intake.
04
Weeks 1–4 after match
Psychological screening
Both surrogate and IPs complete psychological evaluation with a registered professional. Required by most clinics. Surrogates must pass this before medical screening begins.
05
Weeks 2–8 after match
Medical screening
Surrogate completes full medical screening at your fertility clinic. Uterine assessment, bloodwork, infectious disease screening. Do not move to legal until clearance is confirmed.
06
Weeks 4–10 after clearance
Legal agreements
Both parties retain independent lawyers. Agreement is drafted, reviewed, and signed. Allow at least 4–6 weeks. Do not rush. No transfer until this is signed.
07
Month 1 after agreement
Embryo transfer
Surrogate completes a mock or medicated transfer cycle. Embryo transfer takes place. Most families do 1–3 transfer cycles before a positive result.
08
Month 2 after transfer
Confirmed pregnancy
Positive beta HCG confirmed, then first ultrasound at 6–8 weeks. Transition to OB/GYN care after 10–12 weeks.
09
Months 3–11
Pregnancy
Regular OB appointments, anatomy scan at 18–20 weeks, third trimester check-ins. Communication and relationship maintenance throughout.
10
Weeks 34–40
Birth preparation
Hospital notified, birth plan filed, pre-birth order filed (6–8 weeks before due date in applicable provinces), IPs arrange travel and accommodation.
11
Weeks 38–42
Birth
IPs present at birth. Hospital staff briefed. Post-birth paperwork begins immediately. Celebrate.
12
Weeks 1–8 post-birth
Legal parentage and homecoming
Birth certificate, parentage order, passport if needed. Final expense reimbursements processed. Journey complete.

Part Five

Building Your Professional Team

These are the professionals you will need, what they do, and when to engage them. Building the right team early is one of the highest-leverage things you can do.

Surrogacy Agency
Engage at the start
Coordinates matching, supports both parties, manages process. Optional but significantly reduces stress and improves outcomes.
Reproductive Lawyer (IPs)
Before medical screening
Drafts and negotiates surrogacy agreement, obtains parentage order. Must be licensed in the province where the surrogate lives.
Independent Lawyer (Surrogate)
Before medical screening
Reviews and advises on the surrogacy agreement independently. You pay for this. Do not use the same lawyer as the IPs.
Fertility Clinic
Confirm before matching
Medically screens surrogate, manages embryo transfer, handles all clinical coordination. You need this confirmed before matching begins.
Registered Psychologist
After match
Performs psychological evaluation of surrogate and IPs. Required by most clinics. Must have reproductive medicine experience.
OB/GYN
Engage month 3
Manages the pregnancy after the first trimester. Your surrogate may have her own or you find one together.
Life Insurance Broker
During legal phase
Secures life and disability insurance for the surrogate. Confirm surrogacy is covered before transfer.
Financial Advisor / Accountant
During financial setup
Optional but useful for managing trust accounts, cross-border payments, and tax implications.

Part Six

Finding and Matching With a Surrogate

The match is the heart of your journey. Here is what to look for and what to ask.

What Makes a Strong Surrogate Match

Medical eligibility
At least one previous successful pregnancy and birth. Age 21–45. Healthy BMI. No current significant medical conditions.
Lifestyle alignment
Non-smoker, no recreational drug use, low-risk lifestyle. Willingness to follow medical protocols during the pregnancy.
Values compatibility
Aligned views on selective reduction, termination in case of medical necessity, and multi-fetal pregnancies. This conversation must happen before matching.
Communication style
Your surrogate does not need to be your best friend. But you need to be able to communicate openly and honestly throughout the pregnancy.
Support system
A stable home environment, a supportive partner or family, and access to emotional support. Her wellbeing during the pregnancy matters.
Motivation
The best surrogates are motivated by genuine desire to help a family — not primarily by financial need. Ask her why she wants to do this.

Questions to Ask Your Surrogate Match

About her motivation and readiness
  • Why do you want to be a surrogate?
  • What does your partner / support person think about this?
  • Have you talked to your children about what surrogacy means?
  • Have you been a surrogate before? If so, how was that experience?
About values and hard decisions
  • How do you feel about selective reduction if multiple embryos implant?
  • What are your views on termination if a serious medical issue is discovered?
  • Are you comfortable with the number of embryos we plan to transfer?
  • What would you do if you developed a serious health complication?
About the pregnancy relationship
  • How much contact would you like during the pregnancy?
  • Are you comfortable with us attending appointments?
  • How do you feel about being present or having us present at the birth?
  • What does post-birth contact look like for you?
About practical matters
  • Are you comfortable with the reimbursement model and how expenses are managed?
  • Are there any lifestyle restrictions that would be difficult for you to follow?
  • Do you have reliable childcare during medical appointments?
  • What does your support system look like during the pregnancy?

The Legal Agreement Deep-Dive

Your surrogacy agreement is the legal foundation of your journey. Every significant decision about the pregnancy should be addressed in it — before anything medical happens. Here is what must be covered.

Important Notice

The agreement must be signed by all parties before any embryo transfer or medical procedure. This is non-negotiable. Any clinic operating with reputable practices will require confirmation that the agreement is in place before proceeding.

Clause What It Covers
Identity of all partiesFull legal names, addresses, relationship status.
Roles and intentionsClear statement that the IPs are the intended parents and the surrogate has no parental claim.
Medical decisionsWho makes medical decisions during the pregnancy. What happens in a medical emergency.
Selective reductionBoth parties' positions clearly documented.
TerminationUnder what circumstances, if any, termination would be considered. Whose decision it is. What happens if parties disagree.
Number of embryos transferredAgreed number per transfer.
Contact during pregnancyFrequency, type, and boundaries of communication.
Appointment attendanceWhich appointments IPs may attend.
Lifestyle expectationsDiet, travel, activities, substances.
Reimbursement scheduleAll categories, amounts, submission process, payment timeline.
InsuranceConfirmation that life and disability insurance is in place.
Media and social mediaAny restrictions on sharing information publicly.
Post-birth contactWhether the parties intend to stay in touch, and in what way.
If the surrogate withdrawsProcess, financial implications.
If the IPs withdrawProcess, financial implications.
Dispute resolutionHow disagreements will be handled.
Governing jurisdictionWhich province's laws apply.

Part Nine

Clinic and Medical Guide

Choosing Your Fertility Clinic

Questions to ask before you commit:

  • Do you work with surrogates? How many surrogate transfers do you do per year?
  • What is your clinical pregnancy rate per transfer with gestational carriers?
  • What does your surrogate screening process include?
  • Can you receive embryos transferred from another clinic?
  • What is your protocol for embryo transfer — medicated cycle, natural cycle?
  • How many embryos do you recommend transferring?
  • What are your fees for surrogate screening versus a standard IVF cycle?
  • Do you require a psychological evaluation? Who do you recommend?

Medical Screening Process

Screening StepWhat It Involves
Initial consultationReview of medical history, previous pregnancies, current health.
Physical examinationGeneral physical and gynaecological exam.
Bloodwork panelFull CBC, metabolic panel, hormone levels, genetic carrier screening.
Infectious disease screeningHIV, hepatitis B and C, syphilis, CMV, and others. Partner must also be tested.
Uterine assessmentSonohysterogram or hysteroscopy to assess uterine cavity.
Psychological evaluationWith a registered psychologist. Covers motivation, understanding, and mental health history.
Medical clearance letterFormal written clearance from the clinic before legal agreements are signed.

The Embryo Transfer Process

PhaseWhat Happens
Cycle preparation (2–4 weeks)Surrogate takes estrogen to prepare the uterine lining. Monitored by ultrasound and bloodwork.
Lining check (Day 12–14)Ultrasound confirms uterine lining is at target thickness. Transfer date confirmed.
Progesterone start (5–6 days before transfer)Progesterone supplementation begins to prepare for implantation.
Transfer dayEmbryo is thawed and transferred via thin catheter. Usually takes 15–30 minutes. No anaesthesia required.
Beta HCG test (10–12 days post-transfer)Blood test confirms whether implantation occurred.
Second beta HCG (2 days later)Confirms the number is rising appropriately.
First ultrasound (6–7 weeks)Heartbeat confirmed. Transition to OB care begins.

Part Ten

The Pregnancy and Birth

First Trimester — Weeks 1 to 13

  • Weeks 1–4: Beta HCG tests confirm pregnancy and rising levels.
  • Weeks 6–7: First ultrasound. Heartbeat confirmed. This is a milestone to celebrate together.
  • Weeks 8–10: Transition to OB/GYN care. Ensure IPs are added to the approved contact list.
  • Weeks 11–13: First trimester screening (NIPT if desired, nuchal translucency ultrasound).

Second Trimester — Weeks 14 to 27

  • Weeks 14–16: Second trimester begins. Miscarriage risk drops significantly.
  • Weeks 18–20: Anatomy scan. IPs invited to attend if surrogate agrees.
  • Weeks 24–27: Glucose tolerance test. Regular OB appointments continue.

Third Trimester and Birth Preparation — Weeks 28 to 40

  • Weeks 28–32: Growth ultrasound. Group B strep test. Monthly appointments become bi-weekly.
  • Weeks 32–34: IPs begin making travel arrangements. Pre-birth order filed with court (6–8 weeks before due date in applicable provinces).
  • Weeks 34–36: Hospital notified of surrogacy arrangement. Birth plan filed. IPs confirmed on hospital access list.
  • Weeks 36–40: Weekly appointments. IPs on standby near birth location.
  • Birth: IPs present. Celebrate. Take photos. Cry. It is worth everything.

A note on the relationship

How you close the journey with your surrogate matters. A handwritten note, a small gift, or simply a heartfelt phone call acknowledging what she gave your family — these things are remembered. The families who maintain a gracious and grateful relationship at this stage consistently describe their surrogacy experience as one of the most meaningful of their lives.
