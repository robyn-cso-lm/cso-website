# What Happens After You Match With a Surrogate?

When intended parents hear that they have matched with a surrogate, it can feel like the finish line is suddenly in sight.

It is a huge moment. You have found one another. There is relief, excitement, and often a feeling that the journey can finally begin.

And then, sometimes, things get quiet.

There may be medical records to gather, appointments to schedule, legal steps to complete, insurance questions to answer, and clinic timelines that are not always easy to predict. For parents who have already waited a long time, that space between matching and a first transfer can feel surprisingly hard.

The important thing to know is this: a slower-looking period does not mean nothing is happening.

## Matching is the beginning of a careful process

The relationship matters deeply, but the practical foundation has to be built around it.

Before a transfer can move ahead, there may be clinic screening, medical record review, testing, counselling, legal work, and coordination between several people. Each step exists for a reason: to make sure everyone understands the plan and can move forward as safely and clearly as possible.

Some steps happen quickly. Others depend on clinic availability, medical history, or paperwork outside anyone's direct control.

That can be frustrating. It can also be completely normal.

## There is no universal timeline

It is natural to want a precise answer to the question, “How long until transfer?” But no responsible agency can promise one.

Every clinic has its own process. Every surrogate has her own medical history. Legal timelines and treatment cycles vary. A plan may need to shift because of a test result, a medication protocol, a calendar issue, or simply the need to wait for the right window.

None of that means the match was a mistake. Often, it means the team is being thorough.

## The early relationship is taking shape too

This time is not only administrative.

It is also when intended parents and surrogates begin to learn how they will communicate. How often do updates feel helpful? Is a quick text better than a long email? What does support look like when there is no major news to share?

You do not need to become best friends overnight. You do need a relationship where questions can be asked honestly and both sides feel respected.

Small, consistent contact can make a real difference. A simple “thinking of you” or “we know there is a lot happening behind the scenes” can help everyone feel connected without creating pressure.

## It is okay to feel impatient and grateful at the same time

Many intended parents feel guilty for finding this stage difficult. They may think they should simply be grateful to have matched.

But longing for your child does not switch off because paperwork is underway. You can be grateful, hopeful, scared, and impatient all at once. Those feelings do not make you demanding. They make you human.

The key is to bring those feelings into the right conversations rather than letting them turn into assumptions. Ask what the next step is. Ask what is still pending. Ask when it would be reasonable to check in again.

Good support is not about pretending the wait is easy. It is about making sure you do not have to carry it alone.

## A strong start is worth protecting

The period after a match can feel slower than expected because it is designed to be careful. That care protects the surrogate, the intended parents, and the journey you are beginning together.

There will be moments later when you wish the process moved faster too. For now, focus on the next clear step, keep communication kind and steady, and let the foundations be built properly.

The transfer is important. But so is everything that helps you arrive there feeling informed, prepared, and supported.
