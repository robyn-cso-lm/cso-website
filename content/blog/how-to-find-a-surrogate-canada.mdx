Finding a surrogate is not about finding a perfect person. It is about finding the right fit for a very particular journey.

Medical eligibility matters. It has to. But it is only the beginning. The relationship that carries a journey through transfers, appointments, uncertainty, birth planning, and the emotional moments in between needs more than a good medical file. It needs care, clarity, and compatibility.

## Look beyond medical eligibility

The first questions are usually practical: Has she carried a healthy pregnancy? Is she medically cleared? Is the timing right?

Those questions are important. They protect everyone. But they do not tell you how a relationship will feel once it becomes real.

Intended parents should also think about what kind of connection they want. Do you hope for frequent updates, or would a more independent arrangement feel comfortable? Do you want to attend appointments where possible? What does privacy look like to you? What role do you imagine after birth?

There is no single right answer. The problem begins when people assume they want the same thing without talking about it.

## Compatibility is not a soft extra

Compatibility is often treated as something nice to have after the “serious” parts are settled. At CSO, we see it differently.

Shared values, communication styles, boundaries, and expectations are practical parts of a strong match. A surrogate who enjoys regular contact may be a wonderful fit for intended parents who want to feel involved day to day. A surrogate who values clear, respectful boundaries may be a wonderful fit for parents who want a warm relationship without constant contact.

Neither approach is better. What matters is that both sides genuinely agree.

## Questions worth asking early

The best matching conversations are honest, not polished. They make room for the things that can become difficult later.

- How often would we like to communicate, and by what method?
- What updates matter most during treatment and pregnancy?
- How do we handle a disagreement, a delay, or disappointing news?
- What support does the surrogate need from intended parents?
- What involvement feels right around appointments, birth, and postpartum contact?
- Are there beliefs, family dynamics, or lifestyle considerations that should be understood before matching?

You do not need identical answers to every question. You do need the ability to listen, be clear, and work through differences respectfully.

## Red flags to take seriously

It is okay to slow down when something does not feel settled. A match should never move forward because either side is afraid that another opportunity will not come.

Pay attention if conversations feel consistently one-sided, if questions are avoided, or if either side feels pressured to agree before they are ready. It is also worth pausing when expectations about communication, expenses, privacy, or involvement at birth are vague or repeatedly changing.

Slowing down is not a failure. It is often the most caring thing you can do for everyone involved.

## How CSO approaches matching

We begin with the practical foundations: screening, readiness, and the information both sides need to make an informed decision. From there, we look at the human fit.

We help intended parents and surrogates talk about the things that shape a real relationship, not just a profile. We encourage questions. We create room to reflect after an introduction. And we do not believe anyone should be rushed into a match simply because the timing looks convenient on paper.

The right match is thoughtful and mutual. Both sides should feel heard, respected, and able to be themselves.

## The goal is not certainty. It is a strong start.

No one can promise that every part of a surrogacy journey will be easy. But a careful match gives everyone a far better place to begin.

Take the time to ask the real questions. Be honest about what you need. And choose a process that values the relationship as much as the logistics.
